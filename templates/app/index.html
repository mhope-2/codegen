GENERAL INFORMATION ABOUT THE STUDY
The study hopes to determine the level of acceptability of a COVID-19 candidate vaccine for clinical trial and subsequent mass vaccination upon a successful trial.

Currently, non-pharmaceutical preventive methods appear to be the best means to limit further spread since no standard treatment or cure exists but our social structure and nature of interactions threaten the effectiveness and sustainability of these methods. Vaccines are being considered as a possible way of providing protection to susceptible or vulnerable populations. However, any vaccine candidate produced must be taken through clinical trials to ensure that it is able to offer protection and also doesnâ€™t cause any serious side effects. A clinical trial typically goes through four (4) phases;

PHASE I: Usually test a new drug/vaccine for the first time in a small group of people to evaluate a safe dosage range and identify side effects.

PHASE II: Test treatments that have been found to be safe in phase I but now in a larger group of human subjects to monitor for any adverse side effects.

PHASE III: Conducted on a larger population and in different regions and countries, and are often the step right before a new treatment is approved.

PHASE IV: Takes place after country approval and there is a need for further testing in a wide population over a longer time frame.

However, since the advent of the COVID-19 pandemic, a number of concerns have emerged on the use of a vaccine. As with all public health interventions, a major ingredient for success includes the acceptability of any planned intervention at the population level. Some of the factors that may impact acceptability include sociocultural as well as historical dynamics surrounding any event or the development of an intervention. It is therefore important to assess issues of acceptance and awareness around COVID-19 vaccines among the Ghanaian population. Findings from the study could be used to plan educational programs and create awareness among the Ghanaian community.
